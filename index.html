How A Voting System
Can Change An Election

A Visualization By: Jackson Brown, Richard Ding, Brian Luu, and Vincent Wu

There are numerous different voting systems, each with many variations. Our group wanted to see how each voting system would change the outcome of the 2000 Presidential Election. However before we can do that, we need to explain how each voting system works.

Of the many, this visualization will explain the process of the electoral college and first-past-the-post, supplimentary vote, and alternate vote.

The Electoral College and
First Past the Post

To start out, it is important to note that on election day, people aren't voting for who they support as President and Vice President, they are voting for people whom then in turn cast their electoral vote to their respective candidates. That is the essence of the Electoral College; you vote for electors who will vote for are pledged to a parties candidates.

This is important because the number of electoral votes each state has corresponds to how many electoral voters they have. Washington State has twelve electoral votes, this means that there are twelve electors for the different parties in an election that will cast their electoral votes to their party.

On election day this isn't too important to the average voter however. What is shown to each voter is a ballot that asks them to pick the party that has the candidates they would like to see President and Vice President, and pick only one party.

After the popular votes from each voter is counted, each state then has the electors from the winning party, the party with more than 50% of the popular vote, cast their votes to the electoral college. In this way is a 'winner take all' system.

This process happens in each state across the country, as well as in The District of Columbia. Each slate of electors casts their votes and the winner of the election is determined as soon as a party receives over 50% of the electoral votes, or 270 electoral votes.

Additional Comments on First Past the Post:

It is important to note that while FPTP has it's benefits as a voting system, mainly being that it is quick, easy, decently proportional, and commonly produces centrist policies. However it also has rather large negative side effects associated with it.

First Past the Post has been repeatedly shown to converge to a two party system over multiple election cycles. For example, if there were multiple parties in an election, like in the first small multiple below, the lowest winning party members might feel that their votes were wasted and a party they didn't want to win won the highest, the next year they voted with the highest party that most resembles their party values in order to at least have a decent say in they way they think the country should head towards.

This cycle of the lowest winning party not feeling like their votes were worth anything dropping and adding their votes to the highest winning party that they most align with repeats over and over again until there are a very limited, usually two parties left that represent a wide array of individuals in the country. This is what also forces FPTP to form centrist policies, with only two parties, each party has to cater to a large, non-homogenious group of the population, so each party has to form centrist policies in order to make sure they have a chance at winning again next election cycle.
