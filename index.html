How A Voting System
Can Change An Election

A Visualization By: Jackson Brown, Richard Ding, Brian Luu, and Vincent Wu

0There are numerous different voting systems, each with many variations. Our group wanted to see how different voting systems would change the outcome of the 2000 Presidential Election. However before we can do that, we need to explain how each voting system works.

1Of the many, this visualization will explain the process of the electoral college and first-past-the-post, alternate vote, and supplementary vote.

The Electoral College and
First Past the Post

2To start out, it is important to note that on election day, you aren't voting for who you support as President and Vice President. You are voting for individuals who then in turn cast their electoral vote to their respective candidates. That is the essence of the Electoral College; you vote for electors who will vote for a party's candidates.[1]

Electoral Votes Equals Number of Electors

3The number of electoral votes that each state has corresponds to how many electoral voters a party can receive. Washington State has twelve electoral votes meaning that there are twelve electors for the different parties in an election that will cast their electoral votes to their party.[2] Each state's number of electors is determined by adding the number of senators (2) plus their number of representatives.

Pick One, Only One

4On election day, this isn't too important to the average voter. What is shown to each voter is a ballot that asks them to pick the party that has the candidates they would like to see President and Vice President, and pick only one party.

Winner Gets State's Electoral Votes

5After the popular votes from each voter are counted, each state then has the electors from the winning party, the party with more than 50% of the popular vote, cast their votes to the electoral college. In this way, we see a 'winner takes all' system.

Winner at 270

6This process happens in each state across the country. Each slate of electors casts their votes and the winner of the election is determined as soon as a party receives over 50% of the electoral votes, or 270 electoral votes.[3]

Additional Comments on First Past the Post:

7It is important to note that while FPTP has its benefits as a voting system, mainly being that it is quick, easy, decently proportional, and commonly produces centrist policies. FPTP also has rather large negative side effects associated with it.

8First Past the Post has been repeatedly shown to converge to a two party system over multiple election cycles.[4] For example, if there were multiple parties in an election, like in the first small multiple below, the members from the lowest winning party might feel that their votes were wasted whereupon a party that they didn't want to win, won. Because of this, they will vote in the next year with the highest winning party that most resembles their party values - in order to at least have a decent say in the way that they think the country should head.

9This cycle of the lowest winning party adding their votes to the highest winning party - that they most align with - repeats until there are a very limited number of parties (usually two parties) left that represent the wide range of individuals in the country. This is what also forces FPTP to form centrist policies. With only two parties, each party has to cater to a large, non-homogeneous group of the population, so that each party has to form centrist policies to make sure they have a chance at winning again in the next election cycle.

The Alternate Vote

10For simplicity's sake, we will continue using the Electoral College with the Alternate Vote. So like before, in our system, you would be voting for a slate of individual electors who would vote for the party you voted for.

Don't Choose, Rank

11Unlike before, your ballot would instead ask you to rank the parties. Party A, B, and C is ranked third, first, and second respectively on your ballot. However, someone else may rank them first, third, and second.

Win By Recursion

12The Alternate Vote works by using recursion to select a winner. Let's follow Washington under the Alternate Vote. At the start there are four unique parties, and the Democrats have the highest percent of the popular vote but not over 50%.[5]

The Steps of Alternate Vote

13However that leaves %PERCENT OF OTHER VOTES% incomplete as a decision. To fix this, we remove the lowest winning party, go back to the individual's ballots that votes for said party and assign their vote to their second party choice. This puts Democrats above the 50% mark and they are decided the winner of the state. If no party were to receive over 50% of the newly counted vote, this step would repeat until a winner was found.

Winning the Election

14Like before, this would happen in every state and the electors would be determined. Once all states have voted, the electors would cast their votes and the process would end.

Additional Comments on Alternate Vote:

Unlike FPTP, Alternate Vote is extremely costly. When no winner is immediately shown, checking the lowest winning party votes again for each step of the recursion can be time consuming.

Alternate Vote has a few benefits, however. Many consider the greatest benefit to be that individual voters don't have to worry about who their neighbor is voting for. The problem of votes not mattering doesn't occur in Alternate Vote. If a voter's first ranked party doesn't win, it may be their second, or third, or ... favorite party. It additionally means that fringe votes that know they have very different opinions from the rest of the population can still vote without worrying that their voice doesn't have a say.

The Supplementary Vote

Much like Alternate Vote, our version of Supplementary Vote will work with the Electoral College. Many people think of Supplementary Vote as a shortened version of Alternate Vote.

Don't Rank, Pick Two

Unlike the Alternate Vote, your ballot would instead ask you to pick your first choice party and second choice party. Party A, B, and C chosen as not, first, and second respectively on your ballot. However, someone else may rank them first, not, and second.

Win By Single Step

Supplementary Vote works by using a simple check to select a winner. Washington under Supplementary Vote again shows that there are four unique parties, and the Democrats have the highest percent of the popular vote but not over 50%.

Redistribute Second Choice

The differing point from Alternate Vote to Supplementary Vote occurs here. Instead of recursively removing the lowest winning parties from the ballot and redistributing, we remove all but the two most winning parties and redistribute the removed voters' second choices. Even if no party has over 50% of the popular vote, we assign the winner as the party with the highest percent of the popular vote.

Winning the Election

Like before, this would happen in every state and the electors would be determined. Once all states have voted, the electors would cast their votes and the process would end.

Additional Comments on Supplementary Vote:

Supplementary Vote is a sort of middle ground between the two voting systems - quicker and cheaper while also allowing for voters to feel valuable. However, this system can also lead to two parties becoming incredibly close and creating first and second choice parties for individuals with similar beliefs that are resistant to change.
